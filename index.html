Welcome to Science Olympiad! 🌟

New to the team? This page will tell you everything you need to know about how our club works, what competitions look like, and how to prepare so you can hit the ground running.

🧪 What Is Science Olympiad?

Science Olympiad is a national STEM competition where teams of up to 15 students compete across 23 events spanning biology, chemistry, physics, earth science, engineering, and technology. Think of it as a science track meet — every event runs simultaneously, so each person's contribution matters. Riverdale competes in Division C (high school).

🏫 How Our Club Works

The club is student-led by our three leaders — Florian Kroker-McCormack, Rohan Varia, and Luke Orlowski — with faculty support from Ms. Monti. Here's how it typically runs:

  • Club periods: Regular meeting times at school where leaders help coordinate preparation, answer questions, and track progress.
  • Event assignments: Early in the season, leaders assign each member to 2–3 events based on interest and team needs. You'll work with a partner (most events are teams of 2).
  • Independent study: Much of the preparation happens on your own or with your partner — reading rules, building binders, practicing tests, and constructing devices for build events.
  • Build sessions: For build events (like Hovercraft, Boomilever, or Electric Vehicle), the team coordinates lab or workshop time to construct and test devices.

📋 The Three Types of Events

Every Science Olympiad event falls into one of three categories:

  • 📖 Study Events — Written tests on specific science topics (e.g., Astronomy, Disease Detectives, Rocks and Minerals). You typically get to bring a resource binder with notes you've prepared. Success comes from deep knowledge and a well-organized binder.
  • 🔧 Build Events — You design and construct a device before the tournament and test it at competition (e.g., Hovercraft, Boomilever, Electric Vehicle). These require hands-on engineering, extensive testing, and careful calibration.
  • 🧪 Lab / Hybrid Events — Hands-on science tasks performed at the competition, sometimes with a written component (e.g., Forensics, Chemistry Lab, Experimental Design). Lab technique and time management are key.

📅 How to Prepare (A Typical Season)

The season starts in early fall and builds toward the regional tournament in January. Here's what good preparation looks like:

  • Weeks 1–2: Read your event's official rules on soinc.org cover to cover. Visit the scioly.org wiki page for your event — it has study guides and strategy tips.
  • Weeks 3–6: Start building your resource binder (for study events) or prototyping your device (for build events). Work through practice tests from scioly.org/tests.
  • Weeks 7–10: Refine your binder, do timed practice tests, and identify weak areas. Build event devices should be fully assembled and tested multiple times.
  • Final 2 weeks: Run full timed practice. Review everything. Confirm logistics and make sure your materials are packed and ready.

🏆 What Competition Day Looks Like

Tournament day is exciting but hectic. Knowing the flow helps you stay calm and focused. Here's a typical competition day at the regional tournament:

Early Morning — Arrival
The team meets at school or at the host site (Bronx High School of Science for NYC regionals). Everyone brings their materials — binders, devices, calculators, safety goggles, and anything else their events require.
Morning — Opening & First Events
After a brief opening ceremony, events begin running simultaneously across different rooms. You'll head to your assigned room at your event's scheduled time. Events typically last 30–50 minutes.
Midday — Rotating Events
Events run in blocks throughout the day. Between your events, you'll have downtime — use it to review for your next event or support teammates. Stay near the event schedule board for any changes.
Afternoon — Final Events & Awards
The last events wrap up and the tournament ends with an awards ceremony. Top 3 placements per event are recognized, and overall team standings are announced. This is where the whole team experience comes together.

💡 Tips for New Members

  • Start early. The biggest regret from veteran members is not starting preparation soon enough. Even 20 minutes a day makes a huge difference over weeks.
  • Use scioly.org. The wiki, test exchange, and forums are the single best resources available. Thousands of competitors use them.
  • Communicate with your partner. Most events are done in pairs. Coordinate who studies what, divide your binder sections, and practice together.
  • Ask the leaders. Florian, Rohan, and Luke have all competed and can point you to the right resources. Don't be shy — that's what they're here for.
  • Focus on your binder. For study events, a well-organized, tabbed binder with diagrams, tables, and practice problems is often the difference between a good score and a great one.
  • Have fun. Science Olympiad is one of the most rewarding extracurriculars you can do. The friendships and knowledge you build will last well beyond competition season.
